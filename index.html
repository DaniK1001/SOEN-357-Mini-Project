1. Introduction

1.1. Context

This mini project is a UX/UI case study for DoseBuddy, a mobile health companion app. DoseBuddy is designed for people living with chronic health conditions who need to stay organized with daily medication and regular doctor appointments. Even when someone is responsible and trying their best, managing health routines can get tiring and confusing, especially when there are multiple medications, different timing rules, refills to remember, and follow-up visits.

The purpose of this project is to go through a full UX design process and show how user research and design decisions can shape a clearer, more supportive experience. DoseBuddy focuses on helping users set reminders, track medication usage, manage appointments, and communicate with healthcare professionals in one place.

1.2. Problem Statement

Medication and appointment management often breaks down for normal, everyday reasons: people forget, routines change, notifications get ignored, or the information is hard to find when it matters. A common situation is someone wondering, "Did I already take this dose?" or realizing too late that an appointment is coming up.

Many existing tools feel either too basic (just alarms with no context) or too complicated (too many steps, confusing wording, too many screens). Sometimes the experience is spread across different apps, one for reminders, one for a calendar, another portal for messaging, so users end up switching between places. That friction makes it harder to stay consistent. The main problem DoseBuddy addresses is: how can a mobile app reduce the mental load of chronic care and make the routine feel clearer, simpler, and more reliable?

1.3. Design Goals

The design goals for DoseBuddy were set to keep the product realistic, calm, and easy to use. The app should feel predictable and fast, not overwhelming. The main goals are:

  • Support medication adherence: easy reminder setup, clear notifications, and quick logging (taken / skipped / late).
  • Make info easy to check: users should be able to confirm what they took and when without digging.
  • Help users stay on top of appointments: store appointment details, send reminders, and reduce last-minute surprises.
  • Enable simple communication: allow users to send a question or update to a healthcare professional without friction.
  • Keep it accessible: clear language, readable layout, and flows that work for different levels of tech comfort.

1.4. Target Users & Scope

DoseBuddy targets people who manage their own health daily (for example, someone balancing medication routines with a busy schedule) and people who need extra clarity and reassurance (for example, users who prefer larger text, simpler steps, or more confirmation). The project also considers that some users may rely on a family member or caregiver to help organize appointments and medication, even if they are not the main person using the app.

In terms of scope, this report covers the core UX work for the DoseBuddy concept: research approach, two personas, journey maps for each persona, a storyboard, a user flow chart, initial sketches, wireframes, a clickable prototype, and a usability testing section showing feedback and design iterations. The focus is on the design process and the reasoning behind decisions, not building a fully functional medical system.

2. User Research

2.1. Research Goals

Before any design decisions were made, the priority was to understand what people actually go through when managing medications and appointments in their daily lives. It's easy to assume the problems are obvious, but the real picture only comes through when talking to people directly. The research aimed to answer the following questions:

  • How do people currently remember to take their medications, and when does that routine break down?
  • What causes missed doses? Is it forgetfulness? A busy day? confusion about timing, or something else?
  • How do people feel about reminders and notifications? Do they help, or do they just get ignored?
  • Would people actually log their doses, and what would make that feel worth doing?
  • How do people keep track of appointments today, and what tends to slip through the cracks?
  • What would "messaging a healthcare professional through an app" mean to them, and would they use it?
  • Are there accessibility or simplicity needs that should shape the design from the start?

The goal was not to validate assumptions already in place, it was to let the patterns emerge from real conversations and use them to ground every design decision that followed.

2.2. Research Methods

Semi-structured interviews were chosen as the primary research method. This format allows for consistency across participants while still leaving room to follow up on something interesting when it comes up. Each participant was asked the same set of core questions, with light follow-ups depending on where the conversation went.

Interview format:

  • 10 participants (P1-P10)
  • Approximately 6 to 10 minutes each
  • Conducted over voice calls or in person
  • Short notes were taken during each session, focusing on recurring patterns rather than word-for-word transcription

Core questions asked:

  1. Do you take any medication regularly, or do you help someone who does?
  2. What helps you remember to take it, and what tends to make you forget?
  3. How do you feel about phone reminders and notifications in general?
  4. Have you ever been unsure whether you already took a dose or not?
  5. How do you keep track of doctor appointments right now?
  6. If one app could make all of this easier, what would it actually need to do?
  7. Is there anything about health apps, or apps in general, that frustrates you?

Once all interviews were completed, the notes were reviewed and grouped into recurring themes. Some patterns came up across almost every conversation, such as the "did I already take it?" moment and the frustration with notification overload. Those themes were then translated into concrete design implications.

2.4. Key Findings

  • Finding 1: Missed doses happen because routines get disrupted, not because people are careless. Almost every participant described the same pattern in different words: they stay consistent on predictable days, and things fall apart when something changes. Fixed-time reminders don't hold up when the day doesn't go as planned.
  • Finding 2: Nearly everyone has experienced the "wait, did I already take that?" moment. Nine out of ten participants brought this up. For P8, managing insulin-dependent diabetes while driving a bus, getting it wrong has real health consequences.
  • Finding 3: Notifications are largely being ignored. Seven out of ten participants said they regularly swipe away phone notifications without reading them. What participants described as actually effective was a notification specific enough to act on immediately, without needing to unlock the phone.
  • Finding 4: Appointments don't get forgotten entirely, but the details around them do. None of the ten participants had one reliable system for tracking appointments. The biggest gap wasn't missing the appointment itself, it was everything surrounding it, like preparation and post-visit follow-ups.
  • Finding 5: Logging only works if it's genuinely fast. Participants said they'd be willing to log their doses, but only if it required almost no effort. Any design involving typing, multi-step flows, or navigating away from the home screen gets abandoned quickly.
  • Finding 6: People want a low-pressure way to reach their healthcare provider. Most participants said they'd use some form of in-app messaging, but with clear expectations about what it's for: non-urgent notes without having to call the clinic or wait on hold.
  • Finding 7: Accessibility issues with older users are a design problem, not a user problem. P2, P5, and P10 all mentioned struggling with small text, icons without labels, and layouts that assume prior familiarity with how apps work.

2.5. Design Decisions & Implications

Finding Design Decision
Missed doses happen when routines shift Allow flexible reminder windows (e.g. "remind me between 8-10 AM") rather than fixed-time alarms only
"Did I take it?" causes daily uncertainty Show a timestamped dose log on the home screen, one glance should be enough to confirm
Notifications get ignored unless actionable Support lock-screen logging so users can mark a dose as taken without opening the app
Appointment details slip through the cracks Add structured appointment cards with fields for location, prep notes, and post-visit reminders
Logging only happens if it's instant Make "Mark as Taken" the most prominent action on the home screen, one tap, done
Messaging should feel low-stakes Keep communication simple: a short thread tied to a medication or appointment, no chat-style pressure
Accessibility is a core requirement Minimum 16px font size, high contrast, labeled icons throughout, no gesture-only navigation

3. Personas

3.1. Context

The two personas below were created directly from the interview themes and participant profiles. Instead of trying to represent "every" user, they capture the two strongest patterns that kept repeating: a user who is busy and schedule-driven, and a user who needs clarity and reassurance. These personas were used as decision anchors throughout the design.

3.2. Persona 1 - Marcus

Marcus is a 58-year-old city bus driver from Montreal managing Type 1 diabetes and two other daily medications. His rotating shifts make consistency hard, fixed alarms get dismissed, and hectic mornings mean he sometimes can't remember if he took a dose. He needs everything fast: one tap to log, one glance to confirm, and reminders flexible enough to follow his schedule rather than fight it.

3.3. Persona 2 - Noha

Noha is a 67-year-old recently retired teacher managing hypertension with three daily medications. She's organized and takes her health seriously, but loses confidence in an app the moment something is unclear or unlabeled. Her frustration isn't a lack of ability, most apps just aren't designed with her in mind. She needs large readable text, labeled buttons, and the quiet reassurance that she hasn't missed anything.

Visual Style

DoseBuddy follows a calm, card-based design language. Every screen is built around one primary action and one clear piece of information, nothing competes for attention. Cards are well-spaced with generous padding, and each section is clearly separated so the user always knows where they are and what they're looking at. This was a deliberate response to the research finding that cluttered layouts cause users like Noha to lose trust in an app before they've even used it properly.

Colour

One of the more distinctive choices in DoseBuddy is that each tab has its own colour identity, all sharing the same soft, muted tone:

  • Purple: Home tab, primary actions (Taken button, progress bar)
  • Green: Medications tab, confirmed/taken states
  • Orange: Appointments tab, upcoming visit cards
  • Blue: Messages tab, conversation threads
  • Grey: Settings Tab

This system gives users an immediate visual cue about which section they're in, and it creates clear emotional associations. The background stays neutral light grey throughout with white cards, keeping the hierarchy readable at a glance.

Typography

Headings are large, bold, and clearly weighted above supporting information. Timestamps, secondary labels, and helper text are visually de-emphasised through lighter weight and smaller size, but remain readable. Every navigation icon has a text label underneath.

Animations & Interactions

Transitions are kept subtle and quick. The most important interaction, logging a dose, gives instant visual feedback: the timestamp appears immediately in the Today's Doses list and the progress bar updates.

8. Usability Testing

8.1. Testing Plan

The goal of usability testing would be to verify whether DoseBuddy actually feels fast and clear in practice, particularly for the two main persona needs. The test would be conducted as a moderated usability session using the think-aloud protocol.

8.2. User Tasks

  1. Log a dose from the Home screen: "Mark the next dose as taken." (Success = one tap, timestamp updates).
  2. Confirm whether a dose was taken: "You're unsure if you took your evening dose. Check and tell me what the app says."
  3. Find the medication reminder window: "Go to your medication details and tell me what time window the reminder is set to."
  4. Check appointment details: "Find your next appointment and tell me the location and any prep note."
  5. Send a non-urgent message using a quick option: "Message the clinic about a side effect using the quick message buttons."
  6. Change text size in Settings (accessibility check): "Increase the text size in Settings."

8.3. Feedback Summary

Feedback would be analyzed by grouping participant comments and observations into recurring themes, then ranking each issue by severity (High, Medium, Low).

8.4. Design Iterations

High severity issues would be addressed first, followed by Medium, and then Low severity issues for polish. Examples of potential iterations include making "Today's Doses" more visually prominent, renaming confusing buttons like "Skip" to "Not now", and increasing label sizes for accessibility.

9. Conclusion & Reflection

DoseBuddy started with a straightforward question: why do people with chronic conditions still struggle to stay on top of something as routine as taking a pill? Going through the full UX process made it clear that the problem was never really about memory or motivation. It was about friction. Too many steps, too much noise, and tools that weren't built for real life.

The UX process was what made the difference between designing based on assumptions and designing based on actual needs. Talking to real people surfaced the specific pain points that shaped the whole design. The biggest challenge during the process was balancing the needs of two very different users without building two different apps. The solution was to make the core interactions identical (one tap, one glance) but layer in accessibility options through Settings, so the same app can serve both without compromise.

Overall, the process confirmed that good UX isn't about adding more. It's about removing every obstacle between the user and the thing they actually need to do.
